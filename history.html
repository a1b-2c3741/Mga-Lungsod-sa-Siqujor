Siquijor

Siquijor, the capital municipality of the province, was established as a town in 1794, coinciding with the founding of its parish. The island was first sighted by Spaniards in 1565 during Miguel LÃ³pez de Legazpi's expedition and was named "Isla del Fuego" or "Island of Fire" due to the eerie glow from swarms of fireflies in its molave trees. The town has a rich history, including periods under the jurisdictions of Bohol and later Negros Oriental before becoming an independent province in 1971. The capital was officially transferred to Siquijor town in 1972.

San Juan

San Juan, known for its beautiful beaches and vibrant festivals, was organized as a distinct municipality in 1863. The town celebrates the Bugwas Festival annually in honor of its patron saint, St. Augustine of Hippo. The festival, first held in 2006, showcases the town's rich cultural heritage and traditions. San Juan is also home to Capilay Spring Park, a natural spring turned public swimming pool, which serves as a central gathering spot for locals and tourists alike.

Lazi

Lazi, formerly known as Tigbawan, was established as an independent parish on August 8, 1857. The town is renowned for the San Isidro Labrador Parish Church, commonly known as Lazi Church, built in 1884. Adjacent to the church is the Lazi Convent, constructed between 1887 and 1891, which is one of the largest convents built during the Spanish colonial era. Both structures are considered significant historical landmarks and have been declared National Cultural Treasures by the National Museum of the Philippines. The church is also nominated for inclusion in the UNESCO World Heritage Tentative List.

Maria

Maria, originally known as "Kangminya," was renamed in 1880 by Spanish priest Father Ramon Orza in honor of the town's patron saint, Our Lady of Divine Providence. The town is known for its serene environment, hidden springs, and waterfalls. Maria also hosts the Panambal Festival, celebrating the town's traditional healing practices and cultural heritage. The festival attracts visitors from various parts of the country and even abroad, seeking to experience the town's unique blend of spirituality and tradition.

Enrique Villanueva

Formerly known as Talingting, named after a local fish species, Enrique Villanueva was established as a municipality in April 1924. The town was named in honor of Enrique Villanueva, a former governor of Negros Oriental who played a significant role in its establishment. Despite being the least populated municipality in Siquijor, it boasts scenic coastal areas and a vibrant cultural scene. The town's history is deeply rooted in its community's efforts to gain independence and recognition as a separate municipality.

Larena

Larena, originally known as Canoan, was developed into a capital town during the Spanish period due to its strategic location. It served as the cabecera or capital for the whole island of Siquijor. During the American period, the town was renamed Larena in honor of Demetrio Larena, the first Filipino governor of Negros Oriental and Siquijor. Larena remained the provincial capital until 1972 and continues to be a key port and commercial hub, connecting Siquijor to major cities in Central Visayas and Northern Mindanao.
